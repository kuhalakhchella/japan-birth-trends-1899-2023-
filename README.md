# japan-birth-trends-1899-2023-
Analysis of Japan’s postwar baby booms and fertility decline 
READ ME-
This project analyzes Japan’s historical birth patterns from 1899 to 1900. The analysis focuses on fertility booms and drops.
Key events-
-The post-war baby boom (1947–1949) following World War II
-The introduction of birth control policies, including the Eugenic Protection Law (1948) and the legalization of abortion for economic reasons in 1949
-The 1966 Hinoeuma (Fire Horse) fertility shock, which caused a sharp, temporary drop in births
-The second baby boom (1971–1974), driven by the children of the post-war baby boom generation
-The long-term decline in fertility is leading to population aging and decline
The Total Fertility Rate (TFR) remained close to the replacement level (~2.1) from the late 1950s to the early 1970s. Beginning in the mid-1970s, fertility declined rapidly and fell below replacement level.
In 1990, it was revealed that Japan’s TFR for 1989 had dropped to 1.57, lower than the temporary decline observed in 1966 during the Hinoeuma year. This demographic milestone became known as the “1.57 Shock.”
